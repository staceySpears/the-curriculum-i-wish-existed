# Capstone — Your Project, Your Terms

## Purpose

The capstone is where you bring the curriculum together.

It is not a tutorial project. It is a substantial, self-directed software project that demonstrates planning, execution, debugging, testing, deployment, presentation, and reflection.

## What this track teaches

The capstone teaches ownership. You must define a problem, scope a solution, choose tools, manage tradeoffs, build iteratively, respond to feedback, and produce a credible artifact.

## What failure this fixes

The capstone fixes the failure mode where learners complete curriculum projects but never prove they can choose, shape, and finish a project on their own terms.

## Required evidence

1. **Deployed or clearly runnable project** — someone else can run or access your work
2. **Clear README** — explains what it is, why it exists, how to run it, and what tradeoffs you made
3. **Project walkthrough** — recorded or written explanation of what you built and why
4. **Verification strategy** — tests, manual test plan, smoke tests, API checks, or another defensible method proving it works

## Recommended evidence

- Architecture explanation
- Decision notes
- Debugging note
- STAR-L stories
- User or reviewer feedback
- AI disclosure
- Known limitations
- Future improvements

## Primary spines

- Self-evaluation
- Technical communication
- Debugging

All other spines are also active at the capstone. This is the integration point for every habit the curriculum has been building.

## AI policy

AI may be used as a professional collaborator. You may use AI for code generation, refactoring, testing, documentation, and debugging.

The standard rises here. You must be able to explain, verify, modify, and defend everything in your project. If AI introduces a bug, bad abstraction, or hallucinated API, you are responsible for catching it.

Include an AI disclosure note in your project reflection.

See `../../AI_POLICY.md` and `../../spines/ai-collaboration.md`.

## Modules

Work through the capstone in three phases.

1. **Proposal and scope** — define the problem, user, and solution; write a short proposal; get feedback before building
2. **Build and test** — implement iteratively, test as you go, document decisions, keep debugging notes
3. **Polish, deploy, and present** — finalize documentation, deploy, prepare your walkthrough, complete the self-evaluation

## Gate

The capstone gate proves that you can own a project from definition to delivery.

See `gate.md`.

## Capstone definition of done

The capstone is complete when you can:

- explain what you built and why
- demonstrate it running
- walk through the architecture and key decisions
- show evidence of debugging and revision
- identify what you would improve with more time
- reflect honestly on what you learned
- pass the capstone gate
