# Team Happy Path

This document describes what healthy team learning looks like.

It is not a fantasy where nobody struggles. Healthy teams still get confused, stuck, annoyed, blocked, and wrong.

The difference is that healthy teams make the work visible, share context, and repair problems early.

## A healthy team has shared ownership

Everyone should understand the project, not just the part they personally typed.

Shared ownership means:

- tasks are visible
- decisions are documented
- teammates can explain each other's work at a high level
- no one becomes the permanent expert or permanent beginner
- code review spreads knowledge

## A healthy team communicates early

Small misunderstandings become large problems when they stay private.

Healthy teammates say things like:

> I am stuck and need another set of eyes.

> I do not understand this part yet.

> I think this task is bigger than we estimated.

> I disagree with this approach. Can we compare options?

## A healthy team reviews code with care

Code review is not a place to prove superiority.

It is a place to make the work better and share understanding.

Good review comments are specific, respectful, and tied to the code.

## A healthy team rotates learning opportunities

If one person always handles Git, deployment, backend, frontend, testing, or writing, the team is creating silos.

Rotate responsibilities often enough that everyone grows.

## A healthy team handles conflict directly

Conflict is not automatically bad.

Avoided conflict is usually worse.

Healthy teams name the issue, return to the charter, focus on behavior, and decide what changes next.

## Signs the team is on track

The team is probably healthy if:

- everyone can describe the current goal
- blockers are visible
- pull requests receive real review
- quieter teammates are invited in
- decisions are written down
- the team can revise plans without blame
- retrospectives lead to small behavior changes

## The goal

The goal is not just to ship projects.

The goal is for every teammate to become more capable because the team existed.
