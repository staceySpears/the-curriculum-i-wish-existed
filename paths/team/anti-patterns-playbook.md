# Team Anti-Patterns Playbook

Anti-patterns are common unhealthy patterns that make team learning worse.

They are not moral failures. They are signals.

Name them early so the team can repair them before they become normal.

## One-person carry

### What it looks like

One person does most of the coding, Git work, debugging, or decision-making.

The project may move quickly, but the team does not learn evenly.

### Why it is harmful

The strong teammate burns out or becomes controlling. Other teammates lose practice and confidence. The project becomes dependent on one person.

### Repair

Rotate ownership. Pair intentionally. Require others to explain the carried work. Split tasks by learning goal, not just speed.

## Silent confusion

### What it looks like

A teammate does not understand but stays quiet.

### Why it is harmful

The team mistakes silence for agreement. Confusion compounds until the person feels too far behind to ask.

### Repair

Normalize check-ins. Ask each person to summarize the plan. Use phrases like "What part is still fuzzy?" instead of "Does everyone get it?"

## Merge without review

### What it looks like

Code lands without another person reading it.

### Why it is harmful

The team loses a major learning loop. Bugs and unclear decisions become harder to catch.

### Repair

Require review for meaningful changes. Keep PRs small. Use the code review rubric.

## Permanent specialist

### What it looks like

One person always does backend, frontend, testing, Git, deployment, or writing.

### Why it is harmful

The team creates silos. People become dependent instead of capable.

### Repair

Rotate roles by track or project. Pair specialists with learners. Require documentation of repeated tasks.

## Conflict avoidance

### What it looks like

The team avoids disagreement to stay pleasant.

### Why it is harmful

Problems go underground. Decisions become vague. Resentment builds.

### Repair

Return to the charter. Name the specific behavior. Discuss tradeoffs. Decide the next action.

## Review as dominance

### What it looks like

Review comments become harsh, performative, or personal.

### Why it is harmful

People stop sharing early work. Review becomes threatening instead of useful.

### Repair

Use questions. Tie comments to code and standards. Include what works well. Review the work, not the worth of the person.

## Vague done

### What it looks like

Tasks are marked done when someone feels finished, not when the team standard is met.

### Why it is harmful

Work becomes inconsistent and hard to trust.

### Repair

Use the charter definition of done. Require checks, review, and notes before closing meaningful tasks.

## AI substitution

### What it looks like

A teammate uses AI to produce work they cannot explain.

### Why it is harmful

The team may move faster while actual understanding gets weaker. Gates and interviews expose the gap later.

### Repair

Apply `../../AI_POLICY.md`. Require defensibility, disclosure of material AI assistance, and walkthroughs of AI-assisted code.

## Playbook rule

When an anti-pattern appears, do not start with blame.

Start with:

> What behavior are we seeing, what is it costing us, and what will we try differently this week?
