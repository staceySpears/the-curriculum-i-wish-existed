# Timeline

This curriculum is serious work.

The timeline below is an estimate, not a contract. Some learners will move faster because they have prior exposure, stable time, strong review support, or fewer setup issues. Some learners will move slower because they are starting from genuine zero, working around jobs or caregiving, debugging alone, or rebuilding confidence after a bad learning experience.

Do not use the calendar as permission to skip the gate.

A track is complete when you can pass the Track Gate, not when the estimated number of weeks has passed.

## Summary

The curriculum is designed for two main pacing modes.

**Full-time:** 30 to 40 hours per week, usually 8 to 10 months.

**Part-time:** 10 to 15 hours per week, usually 15 to 20 months.

A slower pace is valid. The goal is durable readiness, not speed-running the repository.

## Pacing assumptions

The estimates assume that you are doing the actual work: reading, coding, debugging, testing, revising, writing reflections, completing projects, and passing gates.

They do not assume that every hour is perfectly efficient. Setup breaks. Tests fail. Concepts need a second pass. Life happens.

That is part of the model.

## Full-time path

A full-time learner should expect the curriculum to take about 8 to 10 months.

This pace assumes 30 to 40 hours per week and enough stability to work most weekdays. It also assumes the learner is not merely watching or reading, but producing code, writing tests, completing projects, and doing gate work.

A reasonable full-time rhythm is:

- 4 days per week for new material and exercises
- 1 day per week for review, debugging, writing, and cleanup
- weekly DSA practice
- weekly reflection or self-evaluation
- project and gate work at the end of each track

Full-time does not mean rushing. It means the curriculum is your main work for that season.

## Part-time path

A part-time learner should expect the curriculum to take about 15 to 20 months.

This pace assumes 10 to 15 hours per week. It is designed for people learning around jobs, caregiving, school, health, or other responsibilities.

A reasonable part-time rhythm is:

- 3 to 5 focused sessions per week
- at least one longer session for project work
- weekly DSA practice
- short written notes after debugging sessions
- gate work treated as its own milestone, not squeezed into the final hour

Part-time learners should be especially careful about context loss. If you take several days off, spend the first session back reviewing before pushing forward.

## Slow-burn path

Some learners will move at 5 to 8 hours per week.

That is valid, but the timeline stretches. At that pace, the curriculum may take two years or more.

For slow-burn learners, the biggest risk is not intelligence. It is continuity. You will need stronger notes, smaller milestones, and more frequent review because each session has to reconnect you to the work.

A slow-burn learner should keep a learning log with:

- what you worked on
- what broke
- what you tried
- what finally worked
- what to do first next time

## When to slow down

Slow down when you are passing through material but cannot explain it.

Slow down when tests pass only because you copied a pattern you do not understand.

Slow down when you cannot debug without guessing randomly.

Slow down when you finish a project but cannot describe the tradeoffs.

Slow down when you are relying on AI to move faster than your own understanding.

The curriculum is designed to build durable skill. Moving too quickly creates hidden debt that comes due later.

## When to move faster

You can move faster when you already have real experience with a topic and can prove it through the gate.

Do not skip a track because it looks familiar. Skim the modules, attempt the project, and try the gate. If you can pass the gate honestly, move on.

The gate is the shortcut.

## Review weeks

Review weeks are not failure weeks.

Build them in deliberately. A review week may include:

- revisiting weak modules
- improving old code
- rewriting a README
- adding tests
- doing extra DSA practice
- recording a project walkthrough
- asking for review
- cleaning up Git history or issues

If you are consistently behind the estimate, add review time instead of pretending you are still on pace.

## Gate timing

Each Track Gate should be treated as a separate milestone.

Do not finish the final module of a track and immediately declare the track complete. The gate requires synthesis. It asks whether you can do the work, explain the work, debug or revise the work, and carry the skill into a less familiar context.

For most tracks, reserve at least several days for the gate. For larger tracks, reserve one to two weeks.

## Capstone timing

The capstone is the easiest place to underestimate time.

A good capstone includes planning, implementation, testing, deployment, documentation, revision, and presentation. It should not be scoped as if everything will go right the first time.

The capstone should include at least three phases:

1. Proposal and scope
2. Build and test
3. Polish, deploy, document, and present

If the project is still expanding halfway through the capstone window, reduce scope. Finishing well matters more than proving ambition through unfinished features.

## Timeline rule of thumb

If you are choosing between moving faster and becoming more solid, choose solid.

The point of this curriculum is not to finish files. The point is to become the kind of candidate and engineer whose work can be inspected, explained, tested, revised, and trusted.
